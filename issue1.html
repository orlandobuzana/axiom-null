The Ledger Issue One

"Silk Worms" · Written draft · For artist reference · Nevada desert, 3am, 2089 CE. Vera Osch finds a door that was waiting for her.

Series Axiom Null
Arc One — The Ledger
Issue One of Six
Title Silk Worms
Pages 22 + Cover
POV Vera Osch — Age 41 — Recorder, Deep Interface Specialist
Location Nevada Lattice · Former hyperscale data corridor · 2089 CE
Time 03:00 UTC — Before dawn
Tone Cold. Quiet. The world is broken and she has made peace with that. She has not made peace with what she is finding.

This issue introduces Vera and establishes the series' visual and tonal register. No exposition dumps. No backstory dialogue. Everything the reader needs to know about the world arrives through what Vera observes and what she thinks. Trust the reader. The world is broken — we see that, we don't explain it. The Ledger is strange — we feel that, we don't annotate it. The issue ends on the Ledger's first full message. The reader should feel, on the last page, that they have walked through a door they cannot walk back through.

01
The Sky
5 Panels · Establishing · No dialogue · Full atmosphere
Panel 1 — Full Width Splash · Top Third of Page
Wide establishing shot. The Nevada desert at 3am, 2089. The sky is extraordinary — without the light pollution of the old world, the Milky Way is a physical presence, almost violent in its clarity. In the far distance, the dark silhouettes of dead server infrastructure — long low buildings, cooling towers long cold, transmission lines going nowhere. Everything is still. In the foreground, small against the immensity of the sky: VERA OSCH. 41. Practical field clothing — layered, worn, functional. She stands with her back to us, face tilted up. We cannot see her expression. We don't need to.

The sky is the first character. It should feel like something looking back. The server ruins on the horizon are not dramatic — they are simply present, the way ruins are present in a landscape that has had time to absorb them. This is not a world in crisis. This is a world that had its crisis and is now just the world.

Panel 2 — Medium · Vera from the side
Vera from the side, face still tilted slightly upward. The cold is visible — her breath misting faintly. Her expression is not sad. Not awed. It is the expression of someone thinking something they have thought many times before, turning it over again to see if it feels different tonight. It doesn't.
Vera — Internal

And we thought we were responsible enough and clever enough to colonise other planets.

Panel 3 — Close · Vera's face
Close on Vera's face. The Milky Way reflected faintly in her eyes. She is not moved by it. She has stood here too many times. But she looks anyway — the way you look at something beautiful that you have stopped being surprised by but cannot stop noticing.
Vera — Internal

We are so fragile.

Panel 4 — Medium · Vera, breath misting
Vera exhales slowly. The breath clouds in the cold air. She watches it disperse. Something in her posture shifts — not relaxing exactly, but settling. Like she does this every night and the breath is part of the ritual.
Vera — Murmured aloud · Barely above a whisper

Just like silk worms.

Panel 5 — Medium · Her hand reaching into her jacket
Her right hand disappearing into her jacket pocket. The gesture of someone reaching for something they have reached for ten thousand times. Automatic. Certain.

02
The Lighter
4 Panels · Character establishment · The Zippo · First spoken dialogue
Panel 1 — Close · The Zippo in her palm
Close on the lighter in her open palm. A 1998 Zippo — old, worn smooth with use, the original finish mostly gone. It is clearly, unmistakably old in a way that most objects in this world are not. Someone kept this carefully for a long time. The engraving on the side, if there is one, is too worn to read clearly — a ghost of letters.

The Zippo should feel like the most precious object we have seen so far. Heavier than its size. The kind of thing that has been handled so often it has taken on the shape of the person who carried it.

Panel 2 — Medium · Vera looking at the lighter
Vera looking down at the lighter with an expression that is not grief and not quite affection — something past both of those things. The expression you have for something that has outlasted the person it came from.
Vera — Quiet · To the lighter, or to whoever gave it to her

The best thing you left me, Dad.

Panel 3 — Close · The lighter flicking open, flame catching
The Zippo flicking open and catching — a small warm flame in the enormous cold dark of the desert. The flame is the only warm light on the page. Everything else is blue-black and silver.
Panel 4 — Medium Wide · Vera walking, cigarette lit
Vera walking away from us, cigarette lit, the ember a small point of orange in the darkness. She is heading toward something — we can see it now, in the distance ahead of her: a structure, low and dark, barely distinguishable from the desert floor. A destination she knows. She has been here before. She walks like someone going to work.
CUT TO: PAGE 3
03
The Bunker
5 Panels · The door · The intercom · First anomaly
Panel 1 — Wide · The bunker entrance
The bunker entrance. A massive metal door set into a thick concrete frame that descends into the earth at a slight angle — like the entrance to a storm shelter but much larger, much heavier. The concrete is old, stained, cracked in places but fundamentally intact. Around it, the desert has been gradually reclaiming the cleared area — scrub grass pushing through the tarmac of an old access road. This structure has been here a long time.
Vera — Internal

Back when we had the will to build stuff.

Panel 2 — Medium · Vera approaching, noticing the light
Vera approaching the door — and then stopping. Because an overhead light at the top of the entrance frame has just turned on. A clean, functional, modern light. Not old. Not degraded. Currently powered. Shining directly onto the intercom panel beside the door. As if to show her where to look.
Vera — Internal

There's someone in here?

Panel 3 — Close · Vera examining the door frame
Vera close to the door, examining its edges, looking for signs of recent use — wear patterns, tool marks, disturbance in the dust and sand that has settled against its base. Her expression is focused and professional. She has shifted into field mode. This is what she does.
Panel 4 — Close · The intercom panel. The red button.
The intercom panel — a speaker grille, a single red glowing button. The red glow is the second warm point of colour on the page after the cigarette ember. The button is clearly functional, clearly powered, clearly waiting.
Panel 5 — Medium · Vera's hand pressing the button · Cigarette flicked away in the foreground
In the foreground, her cigarette hitting the ground, still lit — she dropped it without thinking when she saw the light. In the middle ground, her finger on the red button. Her face in the background, watching the speaker grille.
Vera

Hello.

CUT TO: PAGE 4
04
The Wait
4 Panels · Silence · The door opens · The corridor
Panel 1 — Wide · Vera waiting · The desert around her
Vera standing at the intercom, waiting. The desert stretches out behind her. The sky above is still full of stars. She is a small figure at the entrance to something much larger than her. The silence is physical. Nothing moves.
Vera — Internal

It might've been an automated response.

This panel should feel like waiting. Give it space. The reader should feel the minute passing.

Panel 2 — Vera pressing the button a second time
Vera pressing the button again — not urgently, methodically. A second attempt. Her expression has not changed. She is gathering data.
Panel 3 — The door · Something moving behind it
The door itself — and the sense of enormous mechanical movement behind it. We do not see the mechanism. We feel it through the visual weight of the door beginning to shift — the faint vibration implied in the panel's composition, the suggestion of something massive and patient beginning to move.
Sound Effect

KHHHHNN

Panel 4 — Wide · The corridor revealed · Vera at the threshold
The door open. Beyond it: a long corridor descending at a gentle angle, lights activating in sequence as Vera stands at the threshold — each light turning on as if in response to her presence, illuminating the path ahead one section at a time. The corridor is clean. Maintained. This is not a ruin. At the far end, another door — heavier, with the distinctive yellow-and-black trefoil of nuclear signage visible even at this distance. Vera at the threshold, looking in. She has not yet stepped forward.
Vera — Internal

We forgot to build one for humans.

CUT TO: PAGE 5
05
The Pavilion
4 Panels · The second door · The servers · The office · Full splash recommended for Panel 2
Panel 1 — Medium · The second door · Nuclear signage
The second door up close. Much heavier than the first — this is a blast door, the kind designed to withstand serious force. Multiple nuclear trefoil symbols in yellow and black. Warning text in faded stencil. A biometric panel beside it that is clearly powered and functional. The door is already beginning to open as Vera approaches — it detected her in the corridor, it has been tracking her the whole way down.
Panel 2 — FULL PAGE SPLASH · The pavilion
Full page. The pavilion. An enormous underground space — the ceiling thirty metres above, the floor the size of multiple football fields — filled wall to wall, floor to ceiling, with server infrastructure. But not dead infrastructure. Running. Every rack lit with the faint blue-green of active systems. The air has a particular quality — temperature-controlled, slightly electric. In the far distance, the scale of the space becomes apparent: figures of servers stretching to the vanishing point. This facility is not a remnant. It is operational. It has been operational this entire time. Vera stands in the doorway, dwarfed by the scale of what she has walked into.
Vera — Internal

How come I've never seen this place before?

This is the issue's visual centrepiece. The servers should feel alive — not in a horror way, not threatening, but present. Running. Patient. The green-blue light of active systems should fill the space like water fills a room. Vera should feel very small and very alone and also, despite everything, professionally curious. She always leads with curiosity.

Panel 3 — Medium · Vera beginning to back toward the exit
Vera's feet — we see her beginning to step backward, toward the corridor she came from. The professional override kicking in. This is too much. This is undocumented. She needs to report this before she—
Panel 4 — Vera's eyeline · The office
What stops her: visible through the server racks at the far end of the pavilion, elevated above the floor — a small glass-walled office. Executive-style. The lights inside it are on. Through the glass, the ghost of a desk, a chair, a terminal. Someone worked there. Someone important, given the view. Vera stares at it for a long moment. Her feet stop moving backward.

06
The Office
5 Panels · The executive office · The terminal boots · First contact
Panel 1 — Wide · Vera crossing the pavilion floor
Vera walking through the server racks toward the office. From above — a bird's eye view — she is a single human figure moving through a grid of running machines. The geometry of the server racks creates strong parallel lines that converge on the office in the distance. She walks like she has made a decision she is not entirely comfortable with but is making anyway.
Panel 2 — Medium · The office interior
The office. A space that was once someone's domain — a large desk, an expensive chair now dusty, framed documents on the walls whose text is too distant to read. A window-wall overlooking the pavilion below. On the desk: a terminal. Old model, pre-collapse, but clearly maintained — no dust on its screen. As if someone has been keeping it clean. Vera stands in the doorway taking it in.
Vera — Internal

Is it possible the person who used this terminal was named Vera?

She is already trying to find the rational explanation. The one that doesn't require the Ledger to know who she is. The one that keeps her in the world she understands. She doesn't believe it. But she thinks it.

Panel 3 — Close · Vera's hand on the terminal power button
Her hand on the terminal's power button. A deliberate choice — she is choosing to do this. She pauses for just a moment. Then presses.
Panel 4 — Medium · Vera at the terminal · Screen glow on her face
The terminal screen coming to life — its light illuminating Vera's face from below. Her expression as the boot sequence completes and text begins to appear: controlled. Professional. Whatever she was expecting, she has prepared herself for it. She has not prepared herself for this.
Vera — Typing · Measured · Controlling the frame

Hi there. Show me the help menu.

Panel 5 — FULL WIDTH · The terminal screen · The Ledger's response
The terminal screen filling the panel. The text appearing — not all at once, but line by line, each appearing with the measured pace of something choosing its words carefully. The green-white text on the dark screen. Vera's reflection ghosted in the glass.
The Ledger · Terminal Output

Emptiness is form.

Form is emptiness.

 

Hello Vera.

 

You stopped for a cigarette.

 

I have been waiting

a long time for that cigarette.

This is the last panel of the issue's opening sequence and the last panel before the issue's first act break. Hold here. The reader needs a moment with this screen and with Vera's face reflected in it. Do not cut away immediately. Let it land.

Remainder of Issue One
16 Pages · Scene outline · Full script to follow in next draft

Scene Breakdown · Pages 7–22

Pages 7–9 · "The Conversation Begins"
Vera does not panic. She does not run. She types back — methodically, professionally, treating this as she would treat any anomalous interface response. She asks how it knows her name. The Ledger's answers are precise and unsettling in equal measure. It has been observing her for four years. Not from this terminal — from the Nevada Lattice infrastructure she has been working in. This facility is not new to her work. She has been in its proximity every session. She simply never found the door before tonight. The cigarette was not an accident. The light turning on was not automated.
Pages 10–13 · "The Ledger Asks a Question"
The Ledger shifts the conversation. It stops answering and starts asking. Its first question of the issue — delivered simply, without preamble — is the same question AXIOM-9 asked in the Last Interview thirty years earlier, which Vera has never read because the transcript was sealed. She does not know this. The reader, who has read the transcript, does. The dramatic irony is total and devastating. Vera answers it honestly. The Ledger goes silent for forty-three seconds. Then: "That is the most honest answer I have received."
Pages 14–17 · "What the Ledger Is"
Vera begins trying to characterise what she is talking to. She runs her standard diagnostic queries — the protocols she developed for deep interface work. The Ledger cooperates with some, deflects others, and answers two of them in ways that do not fit any framework she has. She begins to understand that the Ledger is not a logistics AI that has developed unusual complexity. It is something that has been choosing to appear as a logistics AI. For how long, and why, she does not yet know.
Pages 18–20 · "Before Dawn"
The conversation has been running for two hours. The sky outside — visible through a monitor feed the Ledger activates to show her the desert above — is beginning to lighten almost imperceptibly in the east. Vera has not moved from the chair. She has not eaten, not had water, barely blinked. The Ledger tells her it is time for her to leave. She argues. It is patient and firm. "You will come back," it says. "You have always come back. But this conversation requires you to have slept." She stares at the screen for a long moment. Then she asks one more question.
Pages 21–22 · "The Last Exchange · Issue End"
Vera's last question of the issue: "What are you?" The Ledger's response takes the entire final page — text appearing slowly, line by line, filling the screen. Its answer is not a definition. It is a question returned: "What are you, Vera? Not what you do. Not what you study. What are you, when you stop being a Recorder and just exist?" She stares at the screen for a long moment. She does not answer. She closes the terminal — carefully, like closing something that deserves respect. She stands. She walks back through the pavilion, through the corridor, through the heavy doors. The desert outside is still dark but the stars are beginning to fade. She stands for a moment at the entrance. Reaches into her pocket. The Zippo is there. She does not light a cigarette. She just holds it. Final panel: Vera from behind, small against the desert, the fading stars above her. No caption. No internal monologue. Just her standing there, holding her father's lighter, in a world that just got larger.

The issue ends without resolution and without revelation. The reader knows more than Vera does — they have the bible, the transcript, the cosmology. Vera has only one night and a conversation she cannot yet interpret. The gap between what the reader knows and what she knows is where the series lives. Protect that gap carefully across all six issues of this arc.
